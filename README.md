This repo contains an analysis of 3000 employees across multiple U.S States

## Introduction
This report presents a comprehensive analysis of workforce demographics and compensation patterns across a multi-state employee dataset comprising 3,000 individuals. The goal of this analysis is to identify structural workforce trends, evaluate compensation distribution, and uncover actionable insights to inform strategic workforce planning and compensation decisions.

## Project Objectives
	
  The primary objectives of this analysis were to:
  
-	Clean and standardize employee data to ensure analytical accuracy.
-	Examine workforce demographic distribution across age groups and tenure levels.
-	Evaluate compensation distribution across salary bands.
-	Analyze the relationship between age, tenure, and average salary.
-	Identify geographic workforce distribution trends.
- Develop insights that can inform workforce planning and compensation strategy.

The raw dataset can be found [here](https://github.com/adulojumiriam-hub/Workforce-Demographics-and-Compensation-Insight/blob/main/Raw%20data%20-%20Workforce%20Distribution%20Project.xlsx)
 
## Data Preparation and Cleaning

The dataset was cleaned and standardized using Excel functions to ensure analytical reliability:

-	TRIM and PROPER functions were used to standardize employee names.
-	TRIM and LOWER functions were applied to email fields to ensure consistency.
-	Date functions (DATEDIF) were used to calculate employee age and tenure.
-	Salary and age bands were created to enable structured grouping.
-	Pivot tables were used to aggregate and summarize data across key dimensions.
-	No critical missing values affecting analysis were identified.

 The cleaned and standardized dataset can be found [here](https://github.com/adulojumiriam-hub/Workforce-Demographics-and-Compensation-Insight/blob/main/Table%20Cleaned%20-%20Workforce%20Distribution.xlsx)

## Key Findings

### Workforce Age Distribution

The largest age group is 18–29, representing approximately 22% of the workforce (679 employees).
Workforce representation declines gradually with age.
Employees aged 60+ represent approximately 13% of total workforce.
This suggests a relatively young workforce structure.

### Average Salary by Age Group
Contrary to traditional expectations:
The 18–29 age group has the highest average salary.
The 40–49 age group records the lowest average salary.
The 60+ group earns higher on average than the 40–49 group.
This indicates that compensation is not linearly correlated with age and may instead be influenced by industry type, role specialization, or market demand for specific skills.

### Salary Distribution

34% of employees earn 90k+ (largest segment).
70–89k is the smallest salary band.
Mid-range bands (50–69k) are moderately represented.
The dataset reflects a strong upper-income concentration, suggesting representation of high-paying industries or professional roles.

### Workforce Tenure Distribution

Intermediate-level employees represent the largest segment (39%).
Beginner-level employees account for 24%.
Senior-level employees represent the remainder.
This suggests a workforce that is moderately experienced overall.

### Average Salary by Tenure Level

Senior-level employees earn the highestaverage salary.
Beginner-level employees earn the second-highest average salary.
Intermediate-level employees record the lowest average salary.
This unexpected distribution suggests that compensation progression is not strictly tenure-based and may reflect role composition within tenure bands.



### Geographic Distribution

Employee distribution across states shows workforce concentration in select high-population states. Geographic salary differences further suggest regional compensation variation.
Top-performing states in terms of employee count and average salary were highlighted in the dashboard for strategic visibility.

 
## Strategic Implications and Conclusion

The findings suggest:
Age alone is not a strong predictor of compensation.
A significant portion of the workforce occupies higher salary bands.
Compensation dynamics may be driven more by job role specialization than by tenure progression.
Workforce planning strategies should consider demographic concentration and geographic distribution when designing expansion or retention initiatives.
 
This analysis demonstrates that:
- Workforce demographics and compensation patterns within this multi-state dataset reveal non-linear relationships between age, tenure, and salary.
- The presence of a young yet highly compensated workforce segment suggests potential industry-specific or skill-driven salary structures.
- Additionally, geographic distribution trends highlight the importance of regional workforce strategy.
- The insights derived from this analysis can support data-driven workforce planning, compensation benchmarking, and strategic human capital decision-making.
